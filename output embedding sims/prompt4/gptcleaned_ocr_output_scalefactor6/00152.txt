Harold Lasswell (1902-1978) was a prominent researcher in the area of propaganda research. He focused on conducting both quantitative and qualitative analysis of propaganda, understanding the concept of propaganda, and discovering the effects of propaganda on the target audience (Rogers, 1994). Lasswell is credited with creating The Meanings communication procedure of content analysis (Rogers, 1994). Generally, content analysis can be defined as, "the investigation of communication messages by counting various codes in a message in order to measure content variables" (Rogers, 1994). Lasswell referred to "Centers of Communication," explaining that a content analysis should take into account the frequency with which certain symbols appear in a message, the direction in which the symbols try to persuade the audience's opinion, and the intensity of the symbols used. By understanding the content of the message, Lasswell (1948) aims to achieve the goal of understanding the "area of influence the content operated from in order to decide" (p. 71).

This method of content analysis is tied strongly to Lasswell (1948) each definitional communication which states, "Who says what in which channel to whom and with what effect" (p. 18). Lasswell's work continued to progress within this area for the remainder of his career.
